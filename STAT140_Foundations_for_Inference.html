POINT ESTIMATES AND SAMPLING VARIABILITY

COMPARTMENTAL EPIDEMIC MODEL EXAMPLE 

## Create a set of 99,500 individuals, where 5% of them get sick
## and 95% do not get sick
pop_size <- 99500
possible_entries <- c(rep("susceptible", 0.95*pop_size),
                      rep("infectious", 0.05*pop_size))

## Sample 1000 entries without replacement
sampled_entries <- sample(possible_entries, size=1000)

## Compute p_hat: count number that become infectious, 
## then divide by sample size
sum(sampled_entries=="infectious")/1000
## [1] 0.042

WHAT WE KNOW SO FAR…

  • Point estimate: sample statistic, observed from sample
  • Parameter: population level quantity we are interested in
  • Error: difference observed from sample to parameter
  • Sample size: number of observations from population, denoted \(n\)

NEW CONCEPTS

  • Sampling error: how much an estimate will tend to vary from one sample to the next
  • Bias: systematic tendancy to over- or under-estimate the true population value

CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM - SAMPLE MEAN (normal)

When observations \(X_1,...,X_n\) are independent and drawn from a normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) will tend to follow a normal distribution with mean \(\mu_{\bar{X}}=\mu\) and standard error \(\sigma_{\bar{X}}=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\) .

CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM - SAMPLE MEAN (general)

When observations \(X_1,...,X_n\) are independent, drawn from a distribution with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), and the sample size is sufficiently large, the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) will tend to follow a normal distribution with mean \(\mu_{\bar{X}}=\mu\) and standard error \(\sigma_{\bar{X}}=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\) .

The sample size is generally “sufficiently large” when \(n \geq 30\).

CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM - SAMPLE PROPORTION

When observations are independent and the sample size is sufficiently large, the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) will tend to follow a normal distribution with mean \(\mu_{\hat{p}}=p\) and standard error \(SE_{\hat{p}}=\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\).

The sample size is generally “sufficiently large” when two conditions are satisfied:

  1. \(np \geq 10\);
  2. \(np(1-p) \geq 10\).

CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM - SAMPLE PROPORTION

In practice \(p\) is unknown. It tends to be reasonable to plug in \(\hat{p}\):

Standard error:

  • \(SE_{\hat{p}}=\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\)

The sample size is generally “sufficiently large” when two conditions are satisfied:

  1. \(n\hat{p} \geq 10\);
  2. \(n\hat{p}(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10\).

INDEPENDENT SAMPLE OBSERVATIONS

  1. Subjects in an experiment that were randomly assigned to treatment groups are independent.
  2. Observations from a simple random sample are independent.
  3. Other cases require your best judgement.

PRACTICE

If \(X \sim Normal(\mu, \sigma)\), then the sampling distribution for the mean \(\bar{X}\) is

  1. approximately \(Normal(\mu, \sigma/\sqrt{n})\) for \(n \geq 30\)
  2. exactly \(Normal(\mu, \sigma/\sqrt{n})\)
  3. approximately \(Normal(\mu, \sigma)\) for \(n \geq 30\)
  4. exactly \(Normal(\mu, \sigma)\)
